What is Malware?

July 16, 2020

Malware = “Malicious Software”

Frequently when we think about threats to our devices like phones or laptops that have the capability of corrupting the system or destroying data, we immediately refer to it as a computer virus. You are not wrong, but it is not entirely accurate to state that. Viruses are not the only threatening, malicious software that cyber attackers utilize to compromise your device for malicious intent. For instance, on top of viruses, attackers may use worms, spyware, adware, keyloggers, or botnets designed to cause damage to a computer or server. Collectively, malware is the umbrella term for malicious software like viruses, worms, Trojans, spyware, adware, or botnets that have harmful intents.

Cyber attackers are always on the move, creating software that can exploit a system’s vulnerability to gain unauthorized access. With the majority of businesses or services migrating to an online presence, cybercriminals are actively looking for loopholes to penetrate a system using malware to infect a system to obtain sensitive personal information.

Malware is not software a user will typically intend to download or purchase. Instead, malware is forced upon vulnerable systems and servers through unethical methods that cyber attackers employ. Common manifestations of malware include a slower system due to the overwhelming amount of data, starting unknown applications, downloading additional malware, rebooting randomly, deleting files, or encrypting user’s data without the user’s authorization.

Malware can easily penetrate and infect a computer or server instantaneously when vulnerabilities like open ports, outdated antivirus/anti-malware software, or weak firewalls exist. This is why it is imperative or organizations to place a heightened emphasis on securing the entirety of their infrastructure. Many entities have implemented security controls like Network Intrusion Detection(IDS) and Prevention systems(IPS) or Security Information and Event Management(SIEM) to monitor and prevent malware from compromising the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of sensitive information.
